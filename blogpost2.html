Tackling Transhumanism

Vidur Saigal - 11/16/2023

   Transhumanism, a philosophical and scientific movement with ancient roots, represents humanity's enduring quest to transcend its natural limitations. This quest, once purely speculative and steeped in religious and mythological narratives like the Epic of Gilgamesh, has evolved into a rigorous pursuit of practical and tangible human enhancements.

   The 20th century marked a pivotal shift in transhumanist thought. J.B.S. Haldane's seminal 1923 essay "Daedalus: Science and the Future" laid the groundwork for the modern transhumanist movement, envisioning a future where advanced sciences, including biotechnology, profoundly augment human biology. Julian Huxley, who is widely regarded as the founder of transhumanism, conceptualized the movement as an avenue for transcending human limitations, not through divine intervention, but through scientific and technological advancements.

   In the 21st century, the confluence of biotechnology and information technology has accelerated transhumanist objectives. The field of biotechnology, particularly in the areas of prosthetics and gene editing, exemplifies this progress. Advanced prosthetics, transcending traditional limitations, now offer enhanced functionalities, blurring the lines between natural and artificial abilities. Similarly, gene editing technologies like CRISPR have unlocked potential pathways to eradicate genetic diseases and possibly even augment human capabilities beyond their natural state.

   The modern realm of genetic engineering has brought forth the controversial concept of "designer babies." This idea, echoing ancient aspirations to transcend human limitations, now finds a practical avenue through modern biotechnologies like CRISPR and in vitro fertilization. The philosophical underpinnings of transhumanism and genetic engineering delve into the realm of reshaping human existence. The ethical debates in this context have been ongoing, with early discussions preceding the genetic engineering revolution of the 1970s. The potential to create a "trans-human" or genetically enhanced person raises profound ethical questions, especially regarding germline modifications that involve changes passed on to future generations.

   Advocates of genetic engineering in humans argue for its potential to prevent diseases and enhance human capabilities, often comparing it to other forms of medical interventions. They suggest that the ability to select traits could lead to improvements in individual lives and overall social advancement. However, opponents raise significant concerns. They argue that such interventions could lead to a loss of humanity, increased social inequality, and unforeseen genetic defects. The ethical stance against genetic engineering often centers on the belief that it constitutes "playing God" and violates individual rights and dignity. This perspective is prevalent among various societal groups, including those with strong religious beliefs​​.

   In the near future, we may see the widespread adoption of gene therapies that greatly reduce the risk of common diseases. Verve Therapeutics' pioneering product, VERVE-101, designed to prevent heart disease by targeting the PCSK9 gene in the liver, represents a significant step in the realm of gene therapy. This therapeutic advancement, potentially administered as a one-time treatment, could redefine how we approach cardiovascular diseases, marking a shift from chronic management to a more definitive solution​​​​.

   Envisioning a future where such treatments are available at birth raises profound ethical and philosophical considerations. For instance, if Verve's gene therapy becomes a standard newborn intervention, it could significantly reduce the prevalence of heart disease, altering the human experience of aging and longevity. However, the implications of such a widespread application are complex. Philosophers like Roberta M. Berry have explored the ethics of germline genetic modification, often focusing on the prospective parents' decisions to engineer their children's genomes for enhanced traits or disease resistance. This approach to 'perfecting' future generations raises questions about the nature of human experience and the value of genetic diversity​​.

   Kantian ethics, for example, would question the universality of applying such gene therapies to all newborns. The maxim of providing gene therapy to every newborn to gain additional opportunities or health benefits may not hold up when everyone has equal access, potentially diminishing the intended advantage and raising questions about the equity and justice of such interventions​​.

   Moreover, the challenges of gene therapy in early life, such as those encountered in trials for conditions like giant axonal neuropathy (GAN), highlight the ethical complexities involved. Decisions about dose selection, fair participant selection, and engagement with patient communities are particularly pertinent when considering interventions in newborns. The irreversible nature of some gene therapies, like in vivo AAV-mediated treatments, adds to these ethical considerations, especially since they can render participants ineligible for future therapies involving the same vector​​.

   Additionally, ethical issues specific to perinatal gene therapy include ensuring benefits and safety for the subjects, obtaining informed consent, and addressing concerns about fairness in participant selection. These considerations become even more crucial when thinking about applying such therapies universally to newborns. The prospect of altering the genetic makeup of individuals before they have the capacity to consent (as in the case of newborns or fetuses) introduces a new layer of ethical complexity

   Modern philosophical perspectives on genetic engineering, as explored by thinkers like Roberta M. Berry, address the complex ethical dilemmas posed by this technology. Utilitarianism, Kantianism, and virtue ethics offer different lenses to understand the ethical ramifications of germline genetic engineering. For instance, Kantian ethics raises questions about the universalizability of actions like genetic modification for personal advantage. The overarching philosophical discourse suggests that while the science of genetic modification advances, the ethical frameworks guiding its application remain a work in progress, with a call for more comprehensive and practical ethical approaches​​.

   The advent of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) has further expanded the transhumanist horizon. AGI, a form of artificial intelligence (AI) with the ability to understand, learn, and apply intelligence to a wide range of problems, presents a future where human cognitive limitations can be overcome. This development aligns closely with transhumanist ideals, promising a future where human intelligence could merge with artificial intelligence, leading to unprecedented levels of cognitive enhancement.

   Transhumanism, therefore, stands at the crossroads of human evolutionary history and the dawn of a new era defined by biotechnological and AI-driven enhancements. It champions the use of technology to improve the human condition, advocating for a future where aging, disease, and even cognitive limitations are no longer insurmountable obstacles. As these technologies continue to evolve, they raise profound ethical, philosophical, and societal questions, challenging our traditional understanding of what it means to be human. In this grand vision of the future, transhumanism offers a framework for navigating these uncharted waters, promising a radical transformation of human capabilities and a redefinition of human potential.

   I believe the progression toward transhumanism is not only inevitable but also fundamentally crucial for the advancement of humanity. The transhumanist movement, advocating for the use of technologies like genetic engineering, AI, and nanotechnology, envisions a future where the human condition is substantially improved. This improvement is not just about extending lifespans or enhancing cognitive abilities; it's about transcending the limitations that have historically defined human existence​​.

   Critics often voice concerns about the potential for greater social inequities, as initially, these technologies may indeed be accessible only to the affluent. However, similar to the historical trajectory of many groundbreaking technologies, I believe these advancements will eventually become more accessible, significantly improving global health and longevity. The moral imperative here, as I see it, is to strive for a future where human suffering, caused by diseases or cognitive limitations, can be alleviated or even eradicated. The argument that such endeavors are tantamount to "playing God" is, in my view, a resistance to progress that overlooks the fundamental human pursuit of improvement and well-being.

   Moreover, the application of transhumanist technologies can provide solutions to global challenges like climate change and neurological disorders, showcasing their potential beyond individual enhancement​​. The criticism that transhumanism could exacerbate social inequalities, highlighted by figures like Francis Fukuyama, should not deter the pursuit of these technologies but rather guide us in creating equitable frameworks for their distribution and use​​.

   Transhumanism represents a significant leap forward in our evolutionary journey, a step that is both inevitable and ethically imperative. As we navigate this path, our focus should be on harnessing these technologies responsibly and equitably, ensuring that their profound benefits are accessible to all, thereby enriching the human experience in ways previously unimaginable.
